# DMAIC — Canonical Reference

A five-phase improvement cycle from Six Sigma, applied to any process where you want measurable, sustained improvement — not just a one-time fix.

---

## The five phases

**Define** — What is this process supposed to do? What does success look like? What does failure look like? If you can't answer these, you can't improve it.

**Measure** — What specific metrics tell you whether it's working? Name them, define how they're collected, establish a baseline. You can't improve what you can't measure.

**Analyze** — When a metric crosses a threshold, what do you investigate? What are the known failure modes? This phase connects measurement to action — without it, you have dashboards nobody looks at.

**Improve** — Hypothesize a fix, test it, measure the result, deploy or revert. Every improvement attempt is an experiment with a clear success/failure criterion. Failed experiments are data, not waste.

**Control** — How do you prevent regression? What monitoring ensures a fix stays fixed? This is the phase most people skip, and it's why most improvements are temporary.

---

## How to use it

1. Pick a process you own.
2. Write one sentence for each phase. If you can't write the Measure sentence, start there — that's your biggest gap.
3. Run the cycle. When you complete an Improve phase, the Control phase begins immediately.
4. The cycle never ends. A process with a complete DMAIC is a process that gets better. A process without one is a process that drifts.

---

## Common mistakes

**Starting at Improve.** You see a problem, you fix it. But without Define and Measure, you don't know if your fix worked or if you solved the right problem.

**Skipping Control.** The fix works today. Next month, the same problem recurs because nobody put a guard in place. Control is what makes gains permanent.

**Measuring everything.** Pick the 2–3 metrics that actually matter. More metrics means more noise and less attention on each one.

**Treating it as a one-time project.** DMAIC is a cycle, not a checklist. The process evolves. The metrics evolve. The thresholds evolve. If your DMAIC spec hasn't changed in 6 months, it's probably stale.

---

## Principles

1. **Mechanisms over philosophy.** "Get better every day" is a wish. A DMAIC cycle with named metrics and regression detection is a mechanism.
2. **Measure before you improve.** Establish a baseline first, even if the numbers are embarrassing.
3. **Control is not optional.** Every improvement without a regression guard is temporary.
4. **Terminal metrics are the only ones that matter.** Intermediate metrics are for diagnosis, not justification.
5. **Failed experiments are data.** Log every attempt. The pattern of failures is often more informative than successes.
6. **Owners own their own DMAIC.** Each owner inventories their own processes and builds their own improvement cycles.
7. **Evolve the framework.** This reference is v1.0. Propose improvements as you learn what works.

---

*The order matters. Define before you Measure. Measure before you Analyze. Analyze before you Improve. Improve before you Control. Most people start at Improve and wonder why nothing sticks.*
